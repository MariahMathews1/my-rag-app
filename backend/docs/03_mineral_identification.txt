TOPIC: Mineral Properties and Identification

WHAT IS A MINERAL?
A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with an orderly crystalline structure and a definite chemical composition or limited compositional range.

MAIN IDENTIFICATION PROPERTIES

Luster
Describes how light reflects from a mineral surface.
- Metallic: looks like polished metal
- Nonmetallic: glassy, pearly, earthy, silky, resinous, or dull

Color
Color can help but is often unreliable because impurities may change a mineral’s appearance.

Streak
The color of a mineral in powdered form.
- Tested by rubbing the mineral on an unglazed porcelain streak plate
- Especially useful for metallic minerals

Hardness
Resistance to scratching, measured with the Mohs scale.

Mohs hardness scale:
1. Talc
2. Gypsum
3. Calcite
4. Fluorite
5. Apatite
6. Orthoclase feldspar
7. Quartz
8. Topaz
9. Corundum
10. Diamond

Useful comparisons:
- Fingernail: about 2.5
- Copper coin: about 3
- Glass: about 5.5
- Steel nail: around 5 to 6

Cleavage
The tendency to break along flat planes of weak atomic bonding.
Important descriptions:
- One direction
- Two directions at about 90 degrees
- Two directions at about 60 and 120 degrees
- Three directions at 90 degrees, called cubic cleavage
- Three directions not at 90 degrees, called rhombohedral cleavage

Fracture
Breakage not controlled by cleavage.
- Conchoidal fracture creates smooth curved surfaces, as in quartz
- Uneven fracture creates rough irregular surfaces

Specific Gravity
A comparison of mineral density to the density of water.
Minerals such as galena feel unusually heavy for their size.

Other Properties
- Magnetism
- Reaction with dilute acid
- Elastic sheets
- Taste
- Smell
- Striations
- Crystal habit

COMMON MINERALS AND DIAGNOSTIC FEATURES

Quartz
- Nonmetallic, glassy
- Hardness 7
- No cleavage
- Conchoidal fracture

Orthoclase Feldspar
- Usually pink, salmon, or white
- Hardness 6
- Two cleavages at right angles

Plagioclase Feldspar
- Commonly white to gray
- Hardness 6
- Two cleavages at right angles
- Fine striations may appear on cleavage surfaces

Calcite
- Hardness 3
- Three cleavages not at right angles
- Reacts strongly with dilute hydrochloric acid

Halite
- Hardness about 2.5
- Cubic cleavage
- Colorless or white crystals
- Salty taste, though tasting lab samples is generally discouraged

Gypsum
- Hardness 2
- Scratched by a fingernail
- One prominent cleavage
- Commonly white or colorless

Muscovite
- Light-colored mica
- One perfect cleavage
- Splits into thin, transparent, elastic sheets

Biotite
- Dark mica
- One perfect cleavage
- Splits into thin, dark, elastic sheets

Olivine
- Olive-green glassy grains
- Hardness greater than glass
- No cleavage

Hornblende
- Dark green to black
- Two cleavages at about 60 and 120 degrees

Pyroxene
- Dark green to black
- Two cleavages at nearly 90 degrees

Magnetite
- Metallic to submetallic
- Black streak
- Strongly magnetic
- High specific gravity

Hematite
- Metallic or earthy
- Reddish-brown streak
- Important iron ore

Galena
- Metallic silver-gray
- Very high specific gravity
- Cubic cleavage
- Gray streak

Pyrite
- Metallic brassy-yellow color
- Greenish-black streak
- Harder than chalcopyrite
- Often forms cubes

Chalcopyrite
- Metallic brassy-yellow
- Greenish-black streak
- Softer than pyrite

MINERAL IDENTIFICATION STRATEGY
1. Decide whether luster is metallic or nonmetallic.
2. Test hardness.
3. Examine cleavage or fracture.
4. Check streak.
5. Use color, density, magnetism, acid reaction, or special features.
6. Match the complete property set instead of relying on one property alone.
