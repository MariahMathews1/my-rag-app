TOPIC: Geologic Time and Relative Dating

GEOLOGIC TIME
Earth history is divided into eons, eras, periods, epochs, and ages.

Major divisions:
- Precambrian: most of Earth’s history
- Paleozoic Era
- Mesozoic Era
- Cenozoic Era

RELATIVE DATING
Relative dating determines the order of events without assigning exact numerical ages.

Principle of Superposition
In an undeformed sequence of sedimentary rocks:
- Oldest layers are at the bottom
- Youngest layers are at the top

Original Horizontality
Sediments are generally deposited in nearly horizontal layers.

Lateral Continuity
Sedimentary layers originally extend sideways until they thin out or encounter a barrier.

Cross-Cutting Relationships
A fault or intrusion is younger than the rocks it cuts.

Inclusions
Rock fragments included inside another rock are older than the surrounding rock.

Faunal Succession
Fossil organisms occur in a recognizable order through time.

UNCONFORMITIES
An unconformity represents missing geologic time caused by erosion or nondeposition.

Angular unconformity
- Tilted or folded layers below
- Younger horizontal layers above

Disconformity
- Erosion surface between parallel sedimentary layers

Nonconformity
- Sedimentary rock over igneous or metamorphic rock

ABSOLUTE DATING
Absolute dating estimates a numerical age.

Radiometric dating:
- Uses predictable radioactive decay
- Parent isotopes decay into daughter products
- Half-life is the time required for half of the parent atoms to decay

FOSSILS
Index fossils are especially useful for correlation when they:
- Were geographically widespread
- Existed for a relatively short time
- Are easy to recognize

KEY IDEA
Relative dating establishes sequence. Radiometric dating provides numerical ages.
