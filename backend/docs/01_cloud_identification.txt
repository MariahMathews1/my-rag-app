TOPIC: Cloud Identification and Classification

OVERVIEW
Clouds are classified mainly by height above the ground and by appearance or texture. Cloud names often use roots that describe their level or form.

COMMON CLOUD-NAME ROOTS
- cirro-: high-level cloud; often thin and icy
- alto-: middle-level cloud
- strato-: layered or sheet-like cloud
- cumulo-: heaped, puffy, or vertically developed cloud
- nimbo- or nimbus: precipitation-producing cloud

HIGH-LEVEL CLOUDS
High clouds generally occur above about 20,000 feet and are composed mainly of ice crystals.

Cirrus
- Thin, wispy, feathery, or fibrous
- Often appears as hooks, bands, or streaks
- Can be an early sign of an approaching warm front or weather system

Cirrostratus
- Thin, widespread, veil-like layer
- Sun or Moon may appear through the cloud
- Often produces a halo because light is refracted by ice crystals

Cirrocumulus
- Small white patches, ripples, or rows
- Individual cloud elements are very small
- Sometimes called a “mackerel sky”

MIDDLE-LEVEL CLOUDS
Middle clouds usually have bases from about 6,500 to 20,000 feet. They may contain water droplets, ice crystals, or both.

Altostratus
- Gray or blue-gray sheet covering much of the sky
- Sun may appear as a dim or watery disk
- Often indicates an approaching warm front
- Can thicken and lower into nimbostratus

Altocumulus
- Rounded white or gray patches
- Individual elements are larger than cirrocumulus
- May appear in rows or groups
- Morning altocumulus with vertical growth can signal instability later in the day

LOW-LEVEL CLOUDS
Low clouds generally occur below about 6,500 feet.

Stratus
- Uniform gray or whitish layer
- Low, flat, and featureless
- May produce drizzle, light snow grains, or mist
- Fog is essentially stratus at ground level

Stratocumulus
- Low gray or white rolls, patches, or rounded clumps
- Cloud elements are arranged in a layer
- Usually has more texture than stratus

Nimbostratus
- Dark, thick, widespread precipitation cloud
- Produces steady rain or snow
- Usually has little visible vertical development
- The Sun is hidden

VERTICALLY DEVELOPED CLOUDS

Cumulus
- Isolated puffy clouds with flat bases and sharp outlines
- Usually associated with fair weather when small
- Grow upward when warm, moist air rises

Cumulus congestus
- Towering cumulus with strong vertical growth
- May produce showers
- Can develop into cumulonimbus

Cumulonimbus
- Large thunderstorm cloud
- Lightning or thunder confirms cumulonimbus
- May have an anvil-shaped top
- Can produce heavy rain, hail, strong winds, and tornadoes

OTHER CLOUD FEATURES

Wall cloud
- Localized lowering beneath a thunderstorm’s rain-free base
- Associated with the storm updraft
- Rotation may indicate tornado potential

Shelf cloud
- Low horizontal wedge along a thunderstorm outflow boundary
- Often signals strong gusty winds
- Usually not the part of the storm that produces tornadoes

Mammatus
- Pouch-like features hanging beneath a cloud, often an anvil
- Common near strong thunderstorms but do not themselves cause severe weather

Fractus or scud
- Ragged, low cloud fragments
- Often form beneath storms or near fronts
- Can look threatening but are not automatically dangerous

Contrail
- Narrow cloud formed when aircraft exhaust condenses in cold upper-level air

QUICK IDENTIFICATION PROCESS
1. Is lightning seen or thunder heard?
   - Yes: cumulonimbus.
2. Are individual puffy cloud towers clearly visible?
   - Yes: cumulus or towering cumulus.
3. Is the cloud mainly a uniform or broken layer?
   - If the Sun or Moon is a bright patch: cirrostratus or altostratus.
   - If the layer is dark and producing steady precipitation: nimbostratus.
   - If the layer is very low and gray: stratus.
4. Are there white wispy fibers?
   - Yes: cirrus.
5. Are there many small rounded elements?
   - Tiny, grain-like elements: cirrocumulus.
   - Medium rounded elements: altocumulus.
   - Large low rounded elements: stratocumulus.

KEY COMPARISONS
- Cirrocumulus elements are the smallest.
- Altocumulus elements are medium-sized.
- Stratocumulus elements are the largest and lowest.
- Stratus is smooth and layered.
- Cumulus is puffy and vertically developed.
- Nimbostratus gives steady precipitation.
- Cumulonimbus gives thunderstorms.
