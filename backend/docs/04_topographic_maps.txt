TOPIC: Reading USGS Topographic Maps

PURPOSE
A topographic map represents the shape, elevation, and human and natural features of the land.

QUADRANGLE MAPS
A common USGS map is the 7.5-minute quadrangle at a scale of 1:24,000.

Typical map information includes:
- Quadrangle name
- Publication and photorevision dates
- Scale
- Contour interval
- Latitude and longitude along map edges
- Benchmarks and spot elevations
- Roads, buildings, streams, forests, and other features

SCALE
A fractional scale of 1:24,000 means:
- 1 unit on the map equals 24,000 of the same units on the ground
- 1 inch on the map equals 24,000 inches on the ground
- 24,000 inches equals 2,000 feet
- About 2.64 inches on the map equals 1 mile

CONTOUR LINES
A contour line connects points of equal elevation.

Contour interval:
- The vertical elevation difference between adjacent contour lines
- If the contour interval is 10 feet, each neighboring contour differs by 10 feet

Index contours:
- Heavier, labeled contour lines
- Commonly every fifth contour
- With a 10-foot interval, index contours are 50 feet apart

READING SLOPE
- Closely spaced contours indicate steep slopes.
- Widely spaced contours indicate gentle slopes.
- Very few contours indicate nearly flat terrain.

HILLS AND DEPRESSIONS
- Closed concentric contours usually indicate a hill.
- Elevation increases toward the center of a hill.
- Closed contours with inward hachure marks indicate a depression.

STREAM DIRECTION
When contour lines cross a stream, they form a V shape.
- The V points upstream.
- Water flows in the opposite direction.

ELEVATION BETWEEN CONTOURS
If a point lies between the 420-foot and 430-foot contours:
- Elevation is greater than 420 feet
- Elevation is less than 430 feet

BENCHMARKS AND SPOT ELEVATIONS
Benchmark:
- Precisely surveyed elevation
- Often labeled “BM” followed by an elevation

Spot elevation:
- Surveyed elevation at a specific point
- Often printed as a number, sometimes in italics

HIGH AND LOW POINTS
High point:
- Look for the highest closed contour, benchmark, or spot elevation
- Express as a range when the exact summit is unlabeled

Low point:
- Often occurs where a stream exits the map
- Usually expressed as a range between two contour elevations

LATITUDE AND LONGITUDE

Latitude
- Measures distance north or south of the equator
- Lines of latitude run east-west
- 1 degree equals 60 minutes
- 1 minute equals 60 seconds

Longitude
- Measures distance east or west of the Prime Meridian
- Lines of longitude run north-south
- Most locations in the continental United States are west longitude

MAP SYMBOL EXAMPLES
- Green shading: forested land
- Blue: water features
- Brown: contour lines
- Black: many cultural features such as buildings
- Purple or lavender on older maps: photorevisions
- Black square: building or house
- Open square or outlined structure: may indicate a barn or other structure
- Dashed lines: trails, unimproved roads, boundaries, or other mapped features depending on the legend

COMMON TOPOGRAPHIC MAP TASKS
- Identify the quadrangle name
- Record publication and revision dates
- Record scale and contour interval
- Find longitude on the east edge
- Mark a location
- Estimate its elevation
- Find and describe the high and low points
