TOPIC: Earthquakes, Seismic Waves, and Epicenter Location

EARTHQUAKE BASICS
An earthquake occurs when stored elastic energy is suddenly released as rocks break or slip along a fault.

Focus or Hypocenter
- The point inside Earth where rupture begins

Epicenter
- The point on Earth’s surface directly above the focus

Fault
- A fracture or zone of fractures along which movement has occurred

SEISMIC WAVES

P Waves
- Primary waves
- Fastest seismic waves
- Compress and expand material in the direction of travel
- Can travel through solids, liquids, and gases

S Waves
- Secondary waves
- Slower than P waves
- Move material perpendicular to the direction of travel
- Can travel only through solids

Surface Waves
- Travel along Earth’s surface
- Usually cause the strongest shaking and most structural damage
- Include Love waves and Rayleigh waves

USING P-S ARRIVAL TIMES
P waves arrive first because they travel faster. S waves arrive later. The time difference between their arrivals increases with distance from the earthquake.

General process:
1. Record the P-wave arrival time.
2. Record the S-wave arrival time.
3. Subtract to find the S-P interval.
4. Use a travel-time graph to convert the interval into distance.
5. Repeat for at least three seismograph stations.

TRIANGULATION
Each station gives a distance to the epicenter, not a direction.

To locate the epicenter:
- Draw a circle around each station.
- Set each circle radius equal to that station’s calculated distance.
- The point where three circles intersect is the estimated epicenter.

Why three stations are needed:
- One station gives a circle of possible locations.
- Two stations usually give two possible intersections.
- Three stations identify one best location.

EXAMPLE STATION-DISTANCE DATA
A classroom lab may provide distances from a suspected epicenter to stations such as:
- Bellingham
- Edmonton
- Pasadena
- Honolulu

These distances are plotted using the map scale and a compass. The intersection of the plotted circles marks the epicenter.

WAVE VELOCITY
Velocity is calculated as:

velocity = distance / time

Example:
- Convert travel time to seconds.
- Divide distance in kilometers by time in seconds.
- Compare the result to known seismic velocities through different rock types.

Typical interpretation:
- Faster velocities often occur in denser, more rigid rocks.
- P-wave speed varies with mineral composition, density, temperature, pressure, and rock structure.

MAGNITUDE AND INTENSITY

Magnitude
- Measures the energy released by an earthquake
- One value is assigned to the earthquake
- Modern reports commonly use moment magnitude

Intensity
- Describes observed shaking and damage at a particular location
- Varies from place to place
- Depends on distance, ground conditions, and building construction

EARTHQUAKE HAZARDS
- Ground shaking
- Surface rupture
- Liquefaction
- Landslides
- Tsunamis
- Fires and infrastructure damage

PLATE-TECTONIC SETTINGS
- Convergent boundaries can produce very large earthquakes.
- Transform boundaries produce shallow earthquakes from sideways plate motion.
- Divergent boundaries commonly produce shallow earthquakes.
- Intraplate earthquakes can occur away from active plate boundaries.

KEY SUMMARY
An earthquake epicenter is located by comparing P- and S-wave arrival times at multiple stations and triangulating the calculated distances.
